Executive Summary

From a 3,000-Year-Old Field to a Once-in-a-Generation Window

Pokkali Village is a community-owned agritourism initiative anchored by Palliyakkal Service Cooperative Bank (PSCB), situated in the Pokkali wetlands of Ezhikkara and the Kadamakudy Islands, Ernakulam District, Kerala. It is India's first initiative to position a Geographical Indication (GI)-tagged rice ecosystem as the centre of a Responsible Tourism experience — with the farmers, not outside investors, as the principal hosts and beneficiaries.

The project is delivered through three integrated components under one umbrella brand:

Pokkali Village (Umbrella) PAADI Tales (Tour Product) Paadi Veedu (Agro-Ecosystem)
India's first GI-tagged rice tourism destination; 3,000-year heritage; bank-anchored, community-owned, digitally native, foreigner-friendly. A bookable tour product, starting with a 4-hour "Pokkali Discovery" half-day tour, supported by a mobile app for digital-first booking. An 18-month Agro Ecosystem Restoration programme: 51 pilot homes scaling to 2,500 homes; zero-chemical kitchen gardens; a recurring 51-item feast cooked in individual households and served in a common space; closed-loop waste management.

Headline numbers

51 pilot households; 2,500 households at full scale (18 months); a 3,000-year-old rice-prawn farming heritage; a 51-item community feast; zero chemicals, zero waste to landfill; a soft launch on 25 June 2026 in the presence of the District Collector, Ernakulam.

Why now

Kerala recorded 25.8 million tourist visits in 2025, with 8,21,999 foreign arrivals (+11.3% YoY) and the highest foreign footfall in Ernakulam district. Yet Kerala has no dedicated GI-tagged rice tourism village, and the Pokkali cultivation area has shrunk from over 25,000 hectares historically to under 1,000 hectares actively farmed today. Pokkali Village is both a tourism product and a heritage restoration project.

Soft launch, 25 June 2026

A closed-group preview at Ezhikkara with the District Collector as the special guest, featuring a half-day tour walkthrough, a live mobile-app booking demo, and a sample of the 51-item feast.

Section 1

Introduction & Context

1.1 Pokkali — A 3,000-Year-Old Living Heritage

Pokkali (ചവയഭയ) is a unique, salt-tolerant, tall-stemmed rice variety cultivated for over three millennia in the brackish tidal wetlands of coastal Ernakulam, Alappuzha and Thrissur. It is the only rice in Kerala to hold a Geographical Indication (GI) tag, granted in 2008 by the GI Registry of India under the Geographical Indications of Goods Act, 1999. The Pokkali system rotates seven months of paddy with five months of prawn farming on the same land, producing a self-fertilising, 100% organic ecosystem. No chemical fertilisers, no pesticides — ever. The post-harvest rice stalks feed the prawns; prawn excreta and tidal flushing feed the next rice cycle.

The result is a working farm that is also a carbon sink, a biodiversity hotspot (migratory birds, mangroves, native fish) and a climate-resilient food system. The 2018 Kerala floods, which devastated conventional paddy tracts across the state, left Pokkali fields largely unaffected — evidence of the system's inherent climate adaptability.

1.2 The Decline — And Why It Matters

Historically, Pokkali was cultivated across more than 25,000 hectares in coastal Kerala. By the early 2010s this had fallen sharply; today less than 7,000 hectares remain under the rice-prawn system, with fewer than 1,000 hectares actively cultivated. The decline is driven by low returns from rice alone, high labour costs, salinity intrusion, and the gradual exit of farmer families to other livelihoods.

The loss of Pokkali is not merely an agricultural statistic. It is the loss of a 3,000-year-old seed lineage, a coastal ecology, a community knowledge system, and a globally rare food culture. The urgency of revival is matched by the opportunity: an authentic, climate-positive, GI-protected food story that the modern traveller is actively seeking.

1.3 The Responsible Tourism Context

Kerala's Responsible Tourism (RT) Mission, launched in 2017, has grown to 14,000+ registered RT units, 300+ panchayats, and 50+ RT Village clusters across 14 districts. It has distributed more than ₹50 crore annually to local stakeholders and supported 3,000+ women entrepreneurs. The Kumarakom model won the UNWTO Ulysses Award for innovation in governance.

The Kerala Tourism Policy 2023 explicitly calls for "high-value, low-volume" tourism that benefits rural and coastal communities, with a strong emphasis on agritourism, caravan tourism, and heritage. Pokkali Village sits squarely at the intersection of three policy streams: RT Mission (community-led tourism), Kerala Agri Tourism Network (KATN) (farm-linked experiences), and Muziris Heritage Project (living heritage of the Kerala coast).

1.4 Why Pokkali Village, Why Now

  • The market is ready: foreign arrivals to Kerala grew 11.3% in 2025, and Ernakulam records the highest foreign footfall in the state.
  • The product is unique: no other village in India combines a GI-tagged rice, a 3,000-year story, a rice-prawn rotation, and 30-minute access from Kochi International Airport.
  • The community is willing: local households have expressed interest in hosting guests in their homes as RT-certified homestays — a development that transforms Pokkali Village from an attraction into a living village.
  • The institution is credible: Palliyakkal Service Cooperative Bank (PSCB) has been cultivating Pokkali and branding its own rice for years, and has publicly committed "to promote tourism along with agriculture".

2.5 Homestay Hosting — A New Pillar

A significant and recent development: local households have expressed interest in hosting guests in their homes as RT-certified homestays. This is a decisive shift for the project. It means Pokkali Village is no longer an attraction within a village — it is a village where families themselves are the hosts.

Pilot plan (Month 1–6):

  • Identify 10–20 pilot households across one panchayat with prior expression of interest.
  • Support each household through the RT Mission certification pathway: application to local panchayat, inspection, hospitality and English-basics training, RT Unit ID, listing on the Kerala Tourism website.
  • Standardise a revenue-sharing model with the bank as central counterparty: e.g. 70% to host household, 20% to village operations and SHG coordination, 10% to PSCB for platform and quality control.
  • Provide each host with a Pokkali Village host kit: signage, branded linens, safety equipment, single-page English "host story", QR code linking to the mobile app.

Scale plan (Month 7–18):

PhaseTargetGeography
Month 1–610–20 RT-certified homestaysEzhikkara pilot cluster
Month 7–12100+ homestaysEzhikkara + Kadamakudy
Month 13–18250+ homestaysFull Pokkali zone (5 panchayats)

Homestay hosting is the operational expression of the project's community-owned promise. It also unlocks higher-value packages (overnight stays, retreat packages) and protects the project from over-tourism by distributing visitors across many homes rather than concentrating them in a resort-style property.

3.2 Pokkali Discovery — The Flagship Itinerary

The 4-hour half-day tour is designed to be bookable in a single click from the mobile app, with same-day slots when possible. The itinerary:

  1. 0:00 — Welcome at Pokkali Village Centre. Tender coconut, briefing, and a 5-minute orientation to the 3,000-year heritage.
  2. 0:20 — Guided farm walk into the rice-prawn fields with a trained local guide. Visitors see seedling stage or harvest (seasonal), prawn ponds, and the tidal water management system.
  3. 1:30 — Canoe ride through the Pokkali backwater canals. Photography, birdwatching, and an explanation of the carbon-sink and climate-resilience story.
  4. 2:30 — Traditional meal at a host household. A sit-down meal of Pokkali rice, prawn curry, fish thoran, banana-leaf sadya items — the meal is a preview of the recurring 51-item feast described in Section 4.
  5. 3:30 — Farmer conversation. A short, facilitated session with the hosting farmer, with translation support for non-Malayalam speakers. A recipe card and a small packet of Pokkali rice are gifted to each visitor.
  6. 4:00 — Departure with an optional visit to the farm shop for Pokkali rice, prawn pickle, and coir crafts.

The itinerary is fully foreigner-friendly: English and Malayalam audio guides, dietary handling for vegetarians and common allergens, and English-speaking guides trained in a 6–8 week certification programme.

3.3 Mobile App — The Digital-First Booking Engine

The PAADI Tales mobile app is the primary booking channel and the project's digital nerve centre. It is intentionally built mobile-first because the target visitor (domestic weekender, foreign eco-tourist, corporate group) is on a phone.

Core features (MVP at soft launch):

  • Booking engine — tour selection, calendar view, real-time availability, party size, add-ons.
  • Payment gateway — UPI, debit/credit card, and Stripe/PayPal for foreign cards.
  • Interactive map — Pokkali Village layout, host homes, farm shop, restrooms, parking.
  • Audio guides — English and Malayalam narration that plays automatically at 12 marked points on the farm walk.
  • Pre-arrival and post-visit automation — WhatsApp confirmations, day-before reminders, post-visit feedback request, Google + TripAdvisor review prompt.
  • Homestay directory — searchable list of RT-certified host homes with photos, English host story, and instant-book flag.
  • Founding Visitor mode — a "Pokkali Pioneer" badge for the first 50 reviewers, with a 20% lifetime discount and a signed postcard from the farmers.

Tech stack: React Native (single codebase for iOS + Android), Razorpay + Stripe, n8n automation connecting booking → CRM → WhatsApp → email.

Paadi Veedu — Agro Ecosystem Restoration

Paadi Veedu (Paadi Veedu മപലയ മുലം, "Fields and Homes") is the Agro Ecosystem Restoration programme of Pokkali Village. It is the operating system that turns the umbrella project's promise of "community-owned" into a household-by-household reality.

Tagline: "Grown in kitchen-gardens, cooked in home kitchens, served to the world."

4.1 Concept

Paadi Veedu restores the agricultural and ecological base of the Pokkali zone by enabling every participating household to:

  • Grow at least one variety of vegetable, fruit or greens in a zero-chemical kitchen garden.
  • Practise complete green farming — no artificial fertilisers, no chemical pesticides, no hybrid seed.
  • Cook one signature item from the 51-item feast at home, contributing to a recurring community meal.
  • Practise closed-loop waste management — kitchen and agricultural waste composted at the household and reused on the kitchen garden and Pokkali fields.

The unit of change is one home. The scale of change is the village.

4.2 The 51-Household Pilot (Month 1–6)

  • 51 households sign up across Ezhikkara.
  • Each household is assigned one vegetable or fruit variety based on the SHG's kitchen-garden plan (e.g. brinjal, okra, ivy gourd, drumstick, banana, mango, moringa, curry leaves).
  • The household receives a start-up kit: open-pollinated seed, neem cake, vermicompost, a composting bin, a printed calendar of the crop, and a 2-hour kitchen-garden training session.
  • Each household commits to zero chemical input for the duration of the pilot and signs a simple pledge recorded by the SHG.

4.3 The 51-Item Feast

The 51-item feast is the culinary, cultural and commercial expression of Paadi Veedu.

The feast is both a tourist offering and a community ritual. It gives every host household a direct, dignified role in the visitor experience: their kitchen is the production line, their food is the brand.

4.4 Closed-Loop Waste Management

Paadi Veedu is designed to be zero-waste to landfill:

  • Kitchen waste (peels, leaves, plate waste) is segregated at the household, composted in the supplied bin, and converted to vermicompost within 6–8 weeks.
  • Compost is reused on the household's own kitchen garden, with surplus shared with neighbouring Pokkali fields.
  • Pokkali straw and prawn-shell waste are channelled back into the rice-prawn rotation (straw → prawn feed; shell waste → soil conditioner).
  • No single-use plastic is permitted on the site; only steel, glass, banana leaf, and areca palm plates.
  • Grey water from kitchens is routed through a small constructed wetland and reused for garden irrigation.

The result is a visible, demonstrable closed loop that doubles as a learning case study for the foreign eco-tourist, the student researcher, and the corporate CSR group.

4.6 The Responsible Tourism Angle

Paadi Veedu is the most direct expression of the RT Mission's principles within Pokkali Village:

  • Local benefit — every rupee of kitchen-garden produce and feast revenue stays in the household or the SHG.
  • Women-led — SHGs coordinate the feast, the kitchen-garden training, and the food processing.
  • Cultural preservation — traditional recipes, seed varieties, and cooking methods are documented by the SHGs.
  • Environmental sustainability — zero chemicals, zero waste, and a regenerating soil base.
  • Tourist authenticity — visitors see and eat from the same kitchen gardens that feed the village.

Stakeholders & Partnerships

The order below reflects decision-making importance.

6.1 Palliyakkal Service Cooperative Bank (PSCB) — Anchor

  • Role: Project lead, governance, funding, community trust.
  • Why PSCB: A cooperative bank with 80+ years of community standing, an existing Pokkali rice brand, and an explicit institutional commitment to "promote tourism along with agriculture". PSCB's involvement ensures that revenue cycles back to the village rather than to outside investors.

6.2 Pokkali Land Development Agency (PLDA)

  • Role: Land management, agricultural coordination, farmer mobilisation, Pokkali seed-line stewardship.
  • Specific contributions: Coordination of Pokkali farming operations that the tourism product sits within; advisory on seasonality (rice season vs. prawn season vs. fallow); seed bank and heritage-variety documentation.

6.3 District Tourism Promotion Council (DTPC), Ernakulam

  • Role: Tourism promotion, RT Mission coordination, district-level marketing and FAM-trip support.
  • Specific contributions: Inclusion in district tourism circuits, FAM trips for tour operators, RT Village certification support, signage and brochure inclusion, press amplification.

6.4 Ezhikkara Grama Panchayat

  • Role: Local governance, RT Village application, signage, sanitation, local event permissions, waste-management coordination.

6.5 Women's Self-Help Groups (SHGs)

  • Role: Feast coordination, cooking, food processing, kitchen-garden training delivery, souvenirs and value-added products.

6.6 Farmer Cooperatives

  • Role: Day-to-day Pokkali farming, hosting of visitors, on-the-ground guides, training delivery.

6.7 RT Mission Kerala

  • Role: Certification, training, official listing on the Kerala Tourism directory, awards nomination.
  • Specific contributions: RT Village certification for Ezhikkara; RT Unit certification for 250+ homestays; free hospitality and language training; access to the "Best New RT Village" awards cycle.

6.8 Other Partners (Supporting)

  • M S Swaminathan Research Foundation — climate-resilient agriculture storytelling.
  • GI Registry of India — written confirmation of GI logo usage rights for Pokkali Village branding.

Section 10

Call to Action

The Pokkali wetlands are the last working, GI-protected, community-farmed rice-prawn ecosystem in India. They are also 30 minutes from an international airport, in a state that welcomed 8.2 lakh foreign tourists last year, in a district with the highest foreign footfall in Kerala. The opportunity to build a globally distinctive Responsible Tourism product on this foundation is, in practical terms, a once-in-a-generation window.

On 25 June 2026, Pokkali Village invites the District Collector, Ernakulam, to:

  1. Be the Special Guest at the closed soft launch at Ezhikkara, and to experience the half-day tour, the mobile app, and the 51-item feast preview firsthand.
  2. Endorse the RT Village application for the Ezhikkara / Kadamakudy cluster and recommend expedited processing.
  3. Support district-level promotion of Pokkali Village through DTPC Ernakulam, including FAM trips, signage, and inclusion in the Ernakulam district tourism circuit.
  4. Recognise PSCB's anchor role in enabling a community-owned, bank-led model of Responsible Tourism, as a replicable template for cooperative-led heritage tourism across Kerala.
